# Agile Values & Principles

## Overview
The Agile Manifesto (2001) defines 4 core values and 12 principles that underpin all Agile methodologies. It does not prescribe specific processes — it describes a mindset.

## The 4 Values

| We value...                        | Over...                              |
|------------------------------------|--------------------------------------|
| **Individuals and interactions**   | Processes and tools                  |
| **Working software**               | Comprehensive documentation          |
| **Customer collaboration**         | Contract negotiation                 |
| **Responding to change**           | Following a plan                     |

> The items on the right have value, but we value the items on the left more.

## The 12 Principles

1. Satisfy the customer through **early and continuous delivery** of working software.
2. **Welcome changing requirements**, even late in development.
3. **Deliver working software frequently** (weeks, not months).
4. Business people and developers must **work together daily**.
5. Build projects around **motivated individuals** — give them the environment and trust they need.
6. **Face-to-face conversation** is the most efficient channel of communication.
7. **Working software** is the primary measure of progress.
8. Agile processes promote **sustainable development** — a constant pace indefinitely.
9. Continuous attention to **technical excellence** and good design enhances agility.
10. **Simplicity** — maximizing the amount of work not done — is essential.
11. The best architectures and designs emerge from **self-organizing teams**.
12. Teams **reflect and adjust** their behavior regularly to become more effective.

## Key Points
- Agile is a mindset, not a methodology — frameworks like Scrum and Kanban implement it
- The manifesto was a reaction to heavyweight, plan-driven processes (Waterfall, RUP)
- Values are about trade-offs, not absolutes — documentation and planning still matter
- Principle 7 (working software) guards against "almost done" culture
- Principle 12 (reflect & adjust) is the basis for [[Scrum Ceremonies#Retrospective|Retrospectives]]

## When to Use
- Whenever requirements are likely to change or are not fully known upfront
- When rapid feedback loops from stakeholders are possible
- When the team can be co-located or have high-bandwidth communication

## When NOT to Use
- Safety-critical systems requiring exhaustive upfront specification (avionics, medical devices)
- Fixed-scope, fixed-price contracts with zero flexibility
- Solo projects with no stakeholder interaction
